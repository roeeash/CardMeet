CardMeet — meet at the convention you were already going to.

A coordination layer for in-person card-game sales. Buyers and sellers don't agree on a meetup spot — they discover that they'll both be at the same tournament, and the deal happens there. Eight core screens, one structured offer model, and a commitment-window-based reputation system designed to kill the no-show problem.

Screen 01

Onboarding — define the reach

Three questions: where you live, how far you'll travel, and what you play. These three answers fully populate the rest of the app — your calendar, your discovery feed, your match suggestions. Set once at signup, editable in settings.

  • Location is the anchor. Use device GPS by default but make it editable — many users live in one city and play in another. Store as lat/lng, not as a string.
  • Reach is asymmetric to event size. A user might travel 5 km for FNM and 300 km for a Grand Prix. v1 uses a single radius; v2 should let users set per-event-tier reach.
  • Game selection filters everything. A Pokémon-only player should never see a Magic-only convention in their calendar. Multi-select.
  • No account creation friction. This screen sits between basic signup and a "see your calendar" reveal — by step 3 the user already sees value (real conventions populated nearby).
Screen 02

Calendar — your conventions, your RSVPs

The calendar isn't decorative. Every Going / Maybe / No tap directly drives the matchmaking algorithm: a "Going" RSVP says "find me listings I can pick up here." Without RSVPs, the app has no signal — so the UI keeps RSVP one-tap and visible.

  • RSVP is binary at the data layer. Three states (Going, Maybe, No) but only "Going" weighs fully in matching. "Maybe" is a soft signal — surfaced, deprioritized.
  • "+ Add a convention" is essential for v1. You'll have sparse data on day one. User submissions, lightly moderated, fill the calendar faster than scraping ever could.
  • Distance shown per event. Helps users decide whether a far event is worth the trip — and reinforces that the radius is doing real work.
  • Date format is local + relative. "SAT · MAY 9" reads faster than "9/5/2026" and reduces locale confusion across regions.

Screen 03

Browse — surface only what's actually completable

The "Shared con" filter is the killer feature of v1. By default, the feed shows listings whose seller you'll cross paths with at a convention you've already RSVP'd to. Other filters layer on top. This is the discovery experience competitors can't replicate without your data.

  • Shared-con tag is shown per listing. Even when a user removes the filter, the badge keeps reminding them which deals are frictionless to complete.
  • Listing card shows price, condition, set. No fluff. The card image is small because most users recognize cards by name + set, not art.
  • Sort defaults to "Recommended" (shared-con first, then proximity, then recency). "Newest" is available as an explicit sort for power users hunting fresh listings.
  • Card name search is missing here — it lives behind a search bar that pins to the top of the body when scrolling. Card-name search is the second-most-common entry point after the convention filter.
Screen 04

Listing detail — trust signals up front

Three things have to be visible before a buyer commits to negotiation: what is the card, can I trust this seller, and can we actually meet. The seller's no-show count appears next to their rating. The shared-convention banner removes the "where do we meet?" question before it's asked.

  • No-show count is a first-class metric alongside rating. Hiding it would be a moral failure of the design — a seller's reliability matters more than their friendliness.
  • Two CTAs: Buy at asking, or Make offer. Accepting the asking price skips negotiation entirely and creates a match immediately. This is critical — negotiation is friction, not fun, for most buyers.
  • "Make offer" opens a structured form, not a chat. Form has price (required), optional note, and an explicit "submit offer" — it's an event, not a message.
  • Photos paginate horizontally below the hero image. Multiple angles matter for condition assessment. No photo, no listing — enforce in the upload flow.

Screen 05

My Deals — three states, one ball-in-court

This screen is the answer to your problem #3 — messages getting lost in chat. Every active deal has exactly one question: is it your turn or theirs? The turn banner tells you instantly. No reading scroll-back, no missing a message buried under twenty others.

  • Negotiating / Matched / Scheduled are the three lifecycle stages. Completed deals live in a separate Profile → History view to keep this screen focused on action.
  • Turn banner uses warning yellow when it's your turn, neutral grey when waiting. Color carries the urgency; the text confirms it.
  • Buying vs Selling pills let users see at a glance which side they're on without reading the body text. Critical for power users with many active deals.
  • Tab counts double as notification numbers. Push notifications fire on state transitions: counter received, offer accepted, meetup confirmed, meetup reminder.
Screen 06

Offer chain — structured negotiation, not chat

Each offer is a discrete object: a price, an optional note, a timestamp, and a state. Both parties see the same chain. At any moment exactly one person has actions available: Accept, Counter, or Withdraw. There is no parallel conversation that can be missed.

  • Optional note per offer preserves the human texture of negotiation ("noticed slight whitening, ₪220?") without turning the channel into a chat. Notes attach to offers, not to a free-form thread.
  • The action card always shows the legal moves only. If it's not your turn, the card shows "Waiting on Daniel" with a Withdraw option. The interface never lets a user accidentally double-respond.
  • Offers expire after 72h of inactivity (configurable). This prevents zombie deals from cluttering the My Deals screen and keeps social pressure on responders.
  • "Withdraw" is always available to either party. If a buyer changes their mind mid-negotiation, they can exit cleanly without ghosting — preserving the relationship for future deals.

Screen 07

Match → Meetup — the moment everything converges

The instant terms are agreed, the app runs the shared-convention query and presents the strongest match: an event both users marked "Going" to. The pair commits to a 30-minute window — not a single instant — to absorb the natural messiness of convention attendance. The no-show counter only fires if someone fails to honor that window.

  • Best-case is auto-suggested. If multiple shared conventions exist, pick the soonest one with both as "Going." Tie-break by proximity to today.
  • Time defaults to a sensible mid-day slot. Either party can edit. Both must confirm before the meetup is locked in.
  • "Where at venue" is a free-text field for v1. v2 should use a venue-specific picker (Trade Hall, Registration, Food Court) maintained by the convention organizer.
  • No-shared-convention path is reachable via "Other options → Propose a custom location" — covers the edge case where buyer/seller want the deal but have no overlap.
  • Reminder push at T-15min opens a check-in screen for both parties: "Are you at the venue?" Both must check in within their committed window. The window — not convention attendance — defines no-show.
  • Why a window, not a single time? People show up late, leave early, or are at conventions all day. A 30-min window absorbs normal variance while still creating a real commitment to honor.
Screen 08

Check-in — the only moment that defines a no-show

Triggered by push 15 minutes before the committed window opens. Both parties tap a single button: I'm here. The counter starts only if a user fails to check in by the end of their window. Whether they came to the convention at all, when they left, or how long they stayed is irrelevant — the only commitment that matters is the one they made.

  • Single tap, no friction. Geofence verification is intentionally out of scope for v1 — trust the user, escalate via reputation if they lie.
  • "Running late" extends the window by 10 min, once. Mutually agreed. If the other side declines, the original window stands.
  • End of window = ruling moment. If both checked in: deal completes when one taps "Done." If only one checked in: the other gets a no-show. If neither: the deal is voided, no penalty.
  • Post-meetup confirmation follows immediately for users who checked in: "Did the deal happen? Rate the other party." Drives the rating system.

How the screens map to your three problems

The wireframes aren't just feature checklists — each one closes a specific failure mode in the current social-media-chat status quo.

Problem 01
Coordination requires both parties to be online

Solved by the convention layer (Screens 02, 04, 07). The app proposes a shared meetup async; users only need to confirm, not negotiate timing.

Problem 02
No-shows leave sellers hanging

Solved by visible no-show counts (Screen 04) + post-meetup confirmation flow + automatic reputation impact. Bad actors lose visibility fast.

Problem 03
Bargaining gets lost in chat

Solved by structured offers (Screens 05, 06). Each offer is a discrete object with one ball-in-court. No threads to scroll, no messages to miss.
